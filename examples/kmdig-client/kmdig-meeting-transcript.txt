CONTEXT

KMDIG Technologies manufactures industrial compressors and HVAC equipment sold to
factories, hospitals, and data centres across India, SEA, and the Middle East.
They have deployed approximately 14,000 IoT sensors across 2,200 customer sites.
The company wants to build a cloud platform to collect sensor telemetry, run
predictive maintenance models, and surface alerts through a customer-facing portal
and internal operations dashboard.

Their current setup is a patchwork: a basic MQTT broker, a PostgreSQL database
that is already hitting performance limits, and a Python notebook that one of the
data engineers runs manually every Monday to generate a maintenance "at risk" list.

--- 2. Sensor & Telemetry Deep-Dive (Sanjay, Ravi) ---

CURRENT STATE:
- 14,000 sensors across sites. Types: vibration, temperature, pressure, current
  draw, runtime hours.
- Sampling rates vary: vibration at 100Hz (high-frequency, very large), temperature
  and pressure at 1-minute intervals, current draw at 5-second intervals.
- Communication: ~60% of sensors communicate over MQTT via 4G/LTE. ~30% are on
  WiFi (customer-managed, unreliable). ~10% still on RS-485 with local data loggers
  that batch-upload once per hour.
- The MQTT broker is Mosquitto running on a single EC2 t3.medium. Sanjay said:
  "It goes down roughly once a month. We don't have monitoring on it. We find out
  when a customer calls."
- PostgreSQL is on RDS db.t3.large. Current size: 1.1 TB. Query performance is
  "terrible" on anything older than 30 days. No partitioning. No time-series
  optimisation.

OUR QUESTIONS AND THEIR ANSWERS:
Q: What is the expected message volume at full scale?
A (Sanjay): "If all 14,000 sensors push at their max rate, theoretically we'd see
about 400,000 messages per second. Practically, the high-frequency vibration sensors
are only polled on demand — they're not continuously streaming. Realistic sustained
load is probably 2,000–5,000 messages per second."

Q: Do you want to store raw vibration waveforms?
A (Kiran): "Yes. The ML team says they need them for the FFT-based anomaly models.
But we can't store everything forever — maybe 90 days of raw waveforms, longer for
aggregate features."

Q: Is there any edge processing today?
A (Ravi): "No. Everything comes to the cloud raw. We've looked at edge but the
hardware at customer sites is inconsistent — some have Raspberry Pis, some have
nothing. We'd like to add edge processing eventually but it's not in scope for this
phase."

--- 3. Predictive Maintenance Model Discussion (Sanjay, Deepa) ---

CURRENT MODEL:
- A single Random Forest model trained on ~18 months of historical data.
- Features: rolling mean/std of temperature, pressure, and current draw over
  1h, 6h, and 24h windows. Runtime hours since last service.
- Target: binary "failure in next 7 days" (labelled from service records).
- Accuracy: "About 73% precision on the test set. We know it's not great."
- Training is done manually in a Jupyter notebook. No automated retraining.
- No model versioning. The model file is saved to an S3 bucket and overwritten
  each time Sanjay runs the notebook.

WHAT THEY WANT:
- Automated feature engineering pipeline that computes rolling features from raw
  telemetry in near-real-time (within 5 minutes of data arriving).
- A model serving layer that scores every device every hour and writes predictions
  to a database.
- A retraining trigger: either scheduled (weekly) or when model performance degrades.
- Sanjay asked specifically about MLflow: "I've heard of it but never used it. Is
  it the right tool?" We said yes, confirmed experience.
- They want to start with the current Random Forest but want the platform to support
  swapping models without re-engineering the pipeline. Our architect said that's
  exactly what we'd design for — model registry + abstract serving interface.

OUR QUESTIONS:
Q: Do you have labelled failure data? How many failure events?
A (Sanjay): "We have service records going back to 2020. About 840 confirmed failure
events with pre-failure telemetry. We know this is a class imbalance problem."

Q: Who labels new failure events?
A (Deepa): "Our field engineers submit a service completion form. It's manual. The
form goes into a separate CRM (Zoho CRM). We'd need to integrate that to get labels
automatically into the training pipeline."

--- 4. Customer Portal & Alerting (Anita) ---

Anita runs the product side. She described the customer-facing requirements:

- Customers need a web portal (and mobile app eventually) where they can:
  - See all their devices and their current health score
  - View historical telemetry charts (temperature, pressure, vibration summary)
  - Receive push/email/SMS alerts when a device is flagged as high-risk
  - Download a maintenance recommendation report for their site
- The portal needs multi-tenancy: each customer sees only their own devices.
  No exceptions — this is contractual.
- Anita asked about white-labelling: "Some of our larger customers want to embed
  this in their own ERP. Can the portal be white-labelled or accessed via API?"
  We said yes — API-first design with optional white-label UI.
- SLA requirement: the alert must reach the customer within 15 minutes of the
  model scoring a device as high-risk. "Not the next day, not the next hour. 15
  minutes."

Q: How many customers access the portal concurrently?
A (Anita): "Right now? Zero — it doesn't exist. At launch we expect maybe 50
concurrent users. Growing to 500 within 12 months if the product takes off."

Q: Do you have a design system or brand guidelines for the portal?
A (Anita): "Yes. I'll send the Figma files and our brand kit this week."

--- 5. Infrastructure & Cloud (Ravi) ---

  in India — data residency is a hard requirement from some government/PSU customers.

KEY THEMES FOR PROPOSAL (TECHNICAL TEMPLATE)

1. MQTT → Kafka/Kinesis for reliability — the single Mosquitto broker is a
   single point of failure. The first thing we fix.
2. Time-series database — PostgreSQL cannot handle this at scale. TimescaleDB
   or InfluxDB / AWS Timestream to be recommended.
3. Feature store — automated feature engineering, not a Monday notebook.
4. MLflow for model registry and experiment tracking.
5. Alert pipeline with <15-minute SLA — needs async processing, not polling.
6. Multi-tenant architecture — hard requirement, security-critical.
7. API-first portal design — white-label / ERP embedding capability.
8. AWS ap-south-1 only — data residency hard constraint.
9. Full IaC (Terraform) — Ravi's "clickops" comment was a clear ask.
10. Observability stack — they currently fly blind. Prometheus + Grafana + Jaeger.
11. Embedded pairing model — Deepa was explicit. Not a handoff.
12. September 2026 conference deadline — MVP in 6 months from April 1 kickoff.
